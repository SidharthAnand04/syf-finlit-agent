# Credit Cards and Responsible Use

## How Credit Cards Work

A credit card allows you to borrow money from the issuing bank up to a set credit limit to make purchases. At the end of each billing cycle (typically 30 days), you receive a statement showing all transactions and the total balance owed.

If you pay the full statement balance by the due date, you pay no interest. If you carry a balance, the remaining amount accrues interest at the card's Annual Percentage Rate (APR).

## Credit Score Basics

A credit score (FICO score) ranges from 300 to 850 and is calculated from five factors:

- **Payment history (35%)**: Paying on time is the single most important factor.
- **Amounts owed / Credit utilization (30%)**: The percentage of available credit being used. Keeping utilization below 30% is recommended; below 10% is ideal.
- **Length of credit history (15%)**: Older accounts help your score.
- **Credit mix (10%)**: Having a mix of credit types (cards, loans) can help.
- **New credit (10%)**: Opening many new accounts in a short period can temporarily lower your score.

## Choosing a Credit Card

When selecting a credit card, compare:

- APR (interest rate): relevant if you ever carry a balance.
- Annual fee: weigh the fee against rewards value.
- Rewards: cash back, travel points, or store credit. Cash back at 1.5%–2% is straightforward and universally valuable.
- Sign-up bonuses: often worth $200–$500 if spending requirements are met naturally.
- Foreign transaction fees: relevant for international travel.

## Avoiding Common Pitfalls

**Carrying a balance**: Paying only the minimum means most of your payment goes to interest. A $1,000 balance at 24% APR paying only the $25 minimum can take years to pay off and cost hundreds in interest.

**Cash advances**: Typically have higher APRs than purchases and begin accruing interest immediately with no grace period. Avoid unless absolutely necessary.

**Opening too many cards at once**: Each application triggers a hard inquiry that can temporarily lower your score by a few points.

**Missing payments**: A single missed payment can drop your score significantly and trigger penalty APRs. Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment as a safety net.

## Credit Utilization Strategy

To maximize your credit score, try to pay your balance before the statement closing date, not just by the due date. The balance reported to credit bureaus is typically the statement balance. Lower reported balances mean lower utilization and a higher score.

## Secured Credit Cards

A secured credit card requires a cash deposit that becomes your credit limit. It functions like a regular credit card for purchases and reports to the credit bureaus. Secured cards are an effective tool for building or rebuilding credit history.

## Credit Card Rewards Optimization

For simplicity, use a flat-rate 2% cash back card for all purchases. For those willing to manage multiple cards, a tiered strategy uses category-specific cards (e.g., 3%–5% on groceries, dining, or travel) with a flat-rate card as the fallback.

Always ensure the extra rewards earned outweigh any additional annual fees paid.
